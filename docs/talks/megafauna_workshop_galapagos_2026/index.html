Shared framing: how to read the models

Shared framing

How to read the models

Before using maps or species distribution model outputs, we need a shared way to interpret them.

  • Projections are “what-if” futures
  • Uncertainty comes from the full workflow
  • Species distribution models estimate habitat suitability, not certainty
  • Outputs are decision-support tools, not final answers

For this workshop, climate projections help us explore plausible futures, not identify one exact future.

Climate-side uncertainty

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

Climate-side uncertainty

  • Scenario: which future climate pathway?e.g., SSP1-2.6, SSP2-4.5, SSP5-8.5.
  • Climate model: which global model produced the projection?e.g., Earth System Models or Global Climate Models from modeling centers such as GFDL, IPSL, CESM, and others.
  • Run or ensemble member: which realization of the model?e.g., r1i1p1f1, r2i1p1f1, or other ensemble members depending on the model archive.
  • Downscaling approach: how do we translate coarse projections to the regional scale?e.g., delta method, statistical downscaling, and dynamical downscaling.
  • Depth and variable layer: which part of the ocean and which variable?e.g., surface, mid-water, and seafloor layers for temperature, oxygen, salinity, currents, and related predictors.

Biological and species distribution model uncertainty

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

Biological and species distribution model uncertainty

  • Records: where do we have data, and where are gaps?
  • Sampling bias: absent, or just not sampled?
  • Predictors: which variables define the niche?
  • Algorithm: which statistical approach?
  • Suitability: suitable does not always mean present, abundant, or critical habitat.

What species distribution models show

Species distribution models estimate environmental suitability

They do not directly show:

  • confirmed presence
  • abundance
  • reproduction
  • population trends
  • management priority by themselves

Suitable ≠ occupied ≠ important habitat

Why use species distribution models for Blue 5?

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

Why use species distribution models for Blue 5?

Species distribution models are useful here because they integrate species information with physical and biogeochemical ocean conditions to estimate where suitable habitat occurs now, and how that suitability may change under climate change.

Species records
+
Ocean conditions
→
Habitat suitability
→
Climate change impacts
  • Species distribution models link species occurrence records with the ocean conditions that shape habitat suitability.
  • They allow us to translate physical climate change into potential biological responses.
  • For Blue 5, this helps identify where suitable habitats may persist, decline, or shift in the future.

Our baseline species distribution model framework

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

Our baseline species distribution model framework

We use an ensemble species distribution modeling approach that combines multiple algorithms, from classical statistical models to machine-learning methods.

  • Ensemble of 10 algorithms, including GLMs, GAMs, MaxEnt, Random Forest, and Boosted Regression Trees.

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{logit}(p_i) = &\alpha + \beta_T T_i + \beta_S S_i + \beta_O O_{2i} + \beta_{Chl} Chl_i + \beta_V V_i \\ &+ \beta_F FrontFreq_i + \beta_U Upw_i + \beta_E ENSO_t + \varepsilon_i \end{aligned} \]

State variables

Temperature, salinity, dissolved oxygen, chlorophyll, and current velocity.

Ocean variability predictors

Ocean front frequency, upwelling, and ENSO.

Conceptual representation of the predictors used to estimate probability of occurrence or habitat suitability. This is not the exact fitted formula for every algorithm in the ensemble.

What is a physical climate model?

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

What is a physical climate model?

Physical models represent the ocean, atmosphere, land, and ice on numerical grids. They simulate energy and mass exchange through time, producing variables that can be summarized for ecological modeling.

  • horizontal grid
  • vertical layers
  • physical and biogeochemical processes
  • present-day and future time windows

Data readiness is not uniform

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

Data readiness is not uniform

The goal is to make data strengths and limitations visible before modeling decisions are made.

  • Stronger data support more confident model interpretation
  • Sparse data require simpler or more cautious interpretation
  • Biased sampling can shape apparent habitat relationships
  • Expert feedback is needed to prioritize realistic improvements

Interpreting first-generation outputs

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

Interpreting first-generation outputs

When reviewing early species distribution model outputs, focus on whether patterns are ecologically plausible, data-supported, and useful for planning conversations.

  • What patterns seem robust?
  • What patterns need caution?
  • Where does expert knowledge disagree with the model?
  • Which improvements would most strengthen final outputs?

Feedback should be specific

IKI - Save the Blue 5 (SB5)

Feedback should be specific

The most useful feedback identifies what can realistically improve the final outputs within the project timeline.

Data feedback

  • missing sources
  • spatial or temporal gaps
  • questionable records
  • species-specific sampling issues

Model feedback

  • implausible spatial patterns
  • missing predictors
  • scale or depth concerns
  • uncertainty communication
